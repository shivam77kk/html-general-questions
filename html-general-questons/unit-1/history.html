History of the Internet

From ARPANET to today’s global web of information and services.

From Research Networks to the World Wide Web

The Internet began as ARPANET in 1969, a U.S. research project connecting a handful of universities for resilient communication. Over decades, standards such as TCP/IP (1983) enabled different networks to interconnect, forming the modern Internet. In 1991, Tim Berners‑Lee introduced the World Wide Web, making information sharing accessible with browsers, pages, and hyperlinks.

Since then, broadband, mobile, cloud computing, and rich web apps have expanded the Internet’s reach to billions of devices—powering education, commerce, social networks, and entertainment worldwide.

Key Milestones

  • 1969 ARPANET sends the first message between UCLA and SRI.
  • 1971 First email program by Ray Tomlinson popularizes network messaging.
  • 1983 Transition to TCP/IP, defining the core Internet protocol suite.
  • 1990 ARPANET decommissions; the term “Internet” sees broad adoption.
  • 1991 Tim Berners‑Lee launches the World Wide Web at CERN.
  • 2000 Dot‑com era; broadband grows and dynamic web apps emerge.
  • 2007 Smartphones drive the mobile Internet revolution.
  • 2010s Cloud, streaming, and social platforms scale globally.
  • 2020s Edge, 5G, AI‑powered services, and IoT proliferate.
